-- Flashcard study -- 

The plan is to create a flashcard study tool to mainly help with the FE1 exams. These are the 
exams which you must pass to qualify as a solicitor in Ireland. 

Notoriously brutal - there are eight exams which must be passed - each three and a half hours long. The exam subjects are as follows: Contract Law; Constitutional Law; Company Law; Criminal Law; EU Law; Equity and Trustsl Real Property; and The Law of Torts. For each subject, there are around 15-20 distinct topics that might arise. For the more fun subjects, such as constitutional law, there are 28 topics! On an exam paper, eight of those topics will subject to a question. You must answer 5 of those.

The exams sit in March and October each year. Generally it takes at least two sittings to pass all, with most people probably succeeding after three or four attempts. 

The brutality not only comes from the grade required to pass - 50%, but from the harsh marking, which adds marks for points hit, but also subtracts them for points missed! The exams are under the curation of the professional body, The Law Society of Ireland, who make a pretty penny every time you have to resit an exam. It is run almost exclusively by practising solictors who don't want to flood the job market with new solicitors, because that could take away their work, so they carefully curate the numbers in the profession to avoid that. Asking the Law Society to lower barriers is like asking turkeys to vote for Christmas!

Nevertheless, I am lucky enough to have passed all eight and have now managed to qualify as a solicitor, getting through with my hair just about still on. 

The key to passing is unfortunately down to how good your memory is. To give you a sense, a book for one subject is usually 300-400 A4 pages of dense information. You'll probably want to go into the exam with at least 60% of that somewhat on call. 

What makes the information dense is the fact that in Ireland [like the UK, US, Australia etc.], is a common law country, which in practice means that judges have a very important role in interpreting legislation, and can change how a law operates through their decision in an individual case. These cases then form jurisprudence which must be followed. This is opposed to a civil law country such as France or Germany, where laws are heavily codified in legislation, and judges are decision makers more than law makers. 

What this means for students in Ireland is that learning case names, and the facts or legal principles associated with them, is essential. This obviously can be very difficult, as often names have no relationship to the facts - they're just names! 

One of the most famous cases in the world is 'Donoghue vs Stevenson', which was a case where a woman in the early 20th century bought a bottle of root beer and found a snail in it, which poisoned her. It wasn't the fault of the shopkeeper who sold it, as he bought it from the beverage company. However, the woman who drank the beer didn't have any business/contractual relationship with the root beer company. This established the legal principle of 'Duty of Care'. Now how do you make a connection in memory between 'Donoghue', 'Stevenson', 'Snail', and 'Duty of Care'? Yet remembering 'Donoghue v Stevenson' is just child's play compared to EU law! How about the famous case of "Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen", which established the Direct Effect of EU Law; or another prominent case, "Internationale Handelsgesellschaft mbH v Einfuhr- und Vorratsstelle für Getreide und Futtermittel", which concerned the conflict between national and EU laws. 

In an exam, you'll probably fail if you don't walk in armed with at least 100 of the main cases and their associated facts/legal principles. This is where flashcards and quizzes come into play. Forget learning essays off by heart, you need to condense information, like Russian dolls, then blurt it out on a page the second you walk into an exam, so you can constantly refer back to it for hints that unlock your memory. 

By drilling quizzes and recalling cases over and over, anyone can succeed, which is where my application comes into play.
